Computing for the Social Sciences

Course Syllabus Summer 2026

Last updated: June 11, 2026

Welcome to “Computing for the Social Sciences”!

For course information (Emails, Office Hours, etc.) see Home.

This is an applied course for social scientists with little-to-no programming experience who wish to harness growing digital and computational resources. The focus is on learning the basics of programming in R and generating reproducible research. Topics include coding concepts (e.g., data structures, control structures. etc.), data visualization, data wrangling, web-scraping, version control software, exploratory data analysis, and more. Students will leave the course with basic programming skills for the social sciences and the ability to adapt and expand these skills as they encounter new questions, methods, and data beyond the course.

Requirements: No prior experience with R is required, though it’s helpful to have completed a course involving a programming language (e.g., Python, Stata, SPSS, or others) in some capacity. If you’re unsure or have had informal exposure to programming, please email the instructor to see if the course is a good fit.

Note: MACS 30500 is cross-listed with CHDV 30511/ENST 20550/MACS 20500/MAPS 30500/PLSC 30235/PSYC 30510/SOCI 20278/SOCI 40176/SOSC 26032

Learning Objectives

By the end of the course, students will be able to:

  • Construct and execute basic programs in R using programming techniques
  • Use the most important tidyverse libraries
  • Search for external libraries to enhance R’s base functions
  • Apply Git and GitHub workflows for version control within RStudio
  • Create and edit R Markdown documents to produce reproducible reports and presentations
  • Import data from various sources
  • Clean and transform raw data
  • Transform data into a tidy format
  • Visualize and interpret data using descriptive methods
  • Manipulate strings and dates

Materials, Schedule, and Content

The main textbook for this course is the 2nd edition of R for Data Science by Garrett Grolemund and Hadley Wickham. The book is freely accessible online. All course materials and assignments will be available on the course website. Consulting the textbook and reviewing the in-class material is required.

See Course Content and Schedule for information on every lecture. Schedule and Content might be subject to change, but if changes occur students will be promptly informed.

Course Expectations

Intended Audience
This course is for UChicago students who want to use R as a tool for analyzing data, typically in the social sciences. It is not a computer science course, but an introduction to programming for research: many students who have taken it in the past were looking to build skills they could apply to their own research projects. If you have never programmed before, expect a new way of thinking: the course will be challenging but rewarding if you commit for the full quarter.

Own Your Learning Journey
Each class has readings and class materials (slides and code) posted on this website. Come prepared with questions you’d like to raise: class time mixes lecture and practice, and questions are strongly encouraged. Regular attendance is expected. Please inform the instructor in advance if you must miss a class. Assignments may feel challenging, but they are carefully designed and tested to be achievable without AI (see our course-specific AI policies below). Start early, keep up with deadlines, and reach out if you need help along the way!

Trust
Our instructor–student relationship is based on trust. If that trust breaks down, our work shifts away from growth and feedback and toward surveillance and control, which benefits no one. I ask you to trust that I have your best interests at heart and that I have designed this class to support your learning. In turn, I trust that you are here to learn R and you will approach the course seriously. I am also open to discussing and modeling how I use R, with and without AI, in my own work, and to explaining the reasoning behind this course’s policies. You can also expect transparency in grading from me and the TAs. See the Assessment section below for details.

The course includes several types of assessments, each designed to support a different part of your learning:

  • Quizzes: Short weekly quizzes, four in total, will assess your understanding of key concepts, syntax, and coding logic from recent lectures. You will have unlimited attempts to complete each quiz before its deadline, and only your highest score will be recorded. Late quiz submissions will not be accepted.

  • In-Class Participation: You will receive credit for active participation during lectures, such as coding exercises, questions, and other engagement tasks. Class engagement are graded for each lecture. There will be opportunities to participate in every class but you need to complete participation activities for 3 of the 4 class meetings each week of your choice. These activities are designed for live/synchronous participation but may be completed asynchronously within 24 hours of the start of class. Late submissions will not be accepted.

  • Oral Code Checks: Each student is required to sign up for one oral code check. This will be a 10–15 minute, one-on-one conversation with the instructor held toward the end of the course (starting in Week 4). During the check, you may be asked to discuss code you wrote for take-home assignments, explain quiz answers, and connect your work to lectures and class materials. You will be evaluated using a rubric, which will be made available ahead of time. Further details will be provided during the course.

  • Take-Home Assignments: This course has four required take-home assignments covering the main material taught in the course, plus one optional fifth assignment. The optional assignment gives you the opportunity to work on a topic of your own interest. If you submit the optional fifth assignment, you may either skip one of the four required assignments of your choice or use it as an extra submission to replace the grade received on one earlier take-home assignment.

Rubric for Take-Home Assigments

Grades are part of most educational systems, but a score reflects your work on one assignment, not your overall worth or ability. To make grading as transparent as possible, I use rubrics for all graded assessments that require human assessment, such as take-home assignments and oral code checks. Pass/fail assignments and quizzes will not have rubrics.

Specifically, all take-home assignments are evaluated using the following rubric, with scores as Excellent (A+/A), Very Good (A-), Good (B+/B), Needs Improvement (C), or Not Sufficient (below C) across five categories, weighted as follows:

  • Coding Strategy (20%):
    • the logic of your code (how it solves the problem),
    • correctness (does it work?),
    • sophistication (depth of solution),
    • use of course tools/techniques (e.g., conditionals, functions, tidyverse, etc.),
    • efficiency (avoiding unnecessary repetition).
  • Presentation and Explanation (20%):
    • effectiveness of visualizations, tables, or summaries,
    • clarity of conceptual choices in presenting data,
    • your ability to interpret results,
    • use of Markdown syntax.
  • Achievement (20%):
    • your mastery of the required concepts/tools,
    • creativity and initiative,
    • ability to go beyond minimum requirements,
    • note that Excellent in this category indicates truly outstanding work.
  • Reproducibility (20%):
    • compliance with course conventions (repo access, required files submitted),
    • clear use of Markdown/RMarkdown,
    • meaningful and rich history of commit messages,
    • etc.
  • Coding Style (10%):
    • adherence to R code conventions,
    • clarity for others reading your code,
    • appropriate comments (neither too few nor too many),
    • logical organization,
    • use of descriptive variable names.
    • see Why Coding Style Matters.
  • Resources and AI Narrative (10%): Evaluates
    • proper citation of all external resources (links, page numbers, etc.),
    • clear and honest AI narrative (if AI was used), written by you,
    • reflection on what you learned.

To achieve Excellent in a given category, all requirements described above must be met. For the other categories, it might be that only one, or more than one, elements are missing. For example, you could achieve Very Good on “Coding Style” if you are following all coding conventions, but the code is inconsistent, or overly or under commented.

Grading Scheme

Your final grade is given by the cumulative score of all submitted work throughout the course. We use the following grading scheme to determine your final grade: A (95-100), A- (90-94), B+ (87-89), B (83-86), B- (80-82), C+ (77-79), C (73-76), C- (70-72). Grades below 70 are dealt on a case-by-case basis.

Pass/Fail: If you plan to take this course as Pass/Fail, please email me to come up with a shared plan that aligns with your needs and the course expectations. A “Pass” in this course requires a minimum grade of C-. You can choose the Pass/Fail option at any time before the last assignment is graded and final grades are published.

Deadlines and Late Submissions Policies

Deadlines can be found in the course schedule. Typically, all assigments are due at 11:59 PM Chicago time, beside quizzes which are due before class. Specifically:

  • All quizzes are due 1-hour before class of the day the quiz is due (usually on Mondays). Quizzes can be found on Canvas. For quizzes, no late submissions are accepted.

  • All other assignments are due by 11:59 PM Chicago time. Submit on Canvas the link to your GitHub repository. Each assignment (excluding quizzes) includes a 1-hour grace period during which late submissions will not incur a penalty. After this period, a penalty of 1 point will be deducted for every additional 24-hour period the assignment is late. If the assignment is graded as Pass/Fail, no late submissions will be accepted after the 1-hour grace period.

  • Oral code checks will be held in Week 4 and 5 during class time. Students are responsible to sign-up for a slot and respect their sign-up time. Rescheduling requests will be considered only in cases of documented circumstances beyond the student’s control (e.g., serious illness, medical or family emergency) and approval is at the discretion of the instructor.

Missed or Incomplete Submissions

Students are expected to submit all assignments in full and on time. Missed and incomplete submissions are treated as follows:

  • Missed Submissions: We expect students to submit all assignments (e.g., take home work, quizzes, oral checks, and class participation). A missed assignment is worth 0 points (0%) and, as such, will affect your final grade. Please reach out if you miss an assignment due to extraordinary circumstances (illness or emergency). Extensions may be granted on a case-by-case basis. Whenever possible, inform me before the deadline. Things like starting an assignment late or having a busy week does not qualify as an exceptional circumstance. The built-in grace period is meant to give you some flexibility for unexpected issues, but it is not a substitute for starting on time.

  • Incomplete Submissions: For take-home assigments, if more than 25% of a submission is missing, that submission will not be graded and will be treated as a missed submission. If less than 25% is missing, the assignment will be graded with a penalty, depending on the assignment and the amount of it that is incomplete

Academic Integrity & Use of AI

Course-Specific Plagiarism and AI Policies

Collaboration: Collaboration is permitted, but direct copying of someone’s else code is prohibited. Students in this course may help each other by answering questions, brainstorming approaches, and explaining concepts. We all benefit and learn from that. However, (a) you must acknowledge this in your assignment or work; and (b) each student must write, debug, and submit their own code and copy someone’s else code is prohibited.

Use of External Resources: External resources (e.g., beyond course readings, slides, and code shared in class) are permitted but only after having consulted course materials, and must be cited appropriately. If online, copy and paste the link; if offline (e.g., book, article), reference the page or section; if AI, explain how you used it (but see below on AI-specific policies). You are responsible for reporting the sources you consulted and for being able to explain any code or ideas incorporated from these sources.

Use of LLMs and AI: AI tools are powerful, accessible, and increasingly good at writing code. At this point, I am confident that AI tools can solve most of the assignments in this course. But this is a foundational course, and building a programming foundation is work you need to do yourself. The goal is to learn how to think through problems, make mistakes, debug, revise, and understand why your code works or does not. If AI does too much work for you, you may finish the assignment, but you will miss out on much of the learning, as well as the satisfaction that comes from solving coding problems yourself.

For these reasons, AI use (e.g., ChatGPT, PhoenixAI, Copilot, Claude, etc.), is strictly limited to the uses outlined below:

  • First try on your own. Do not copy/paste prompts into AI to get preliminary solutions. You may consult AI only after at least ~15 minutes of independent effort and reviewing class materials.
  • Use only class methods and materials. Your primary solution must use approaches taught in class (slides, book, in-class code). Alternative solutions may be included, and even encouraged, but only as additional work, properly cited, and compared against your primary solution.
  • Disclose use. Every assignment must include a statement, written exclusively by you (no AI-editing, etc.), describing how (if at all) you used AI, for which task, how you validated and integrated AI output with course sources, and what you learned in the process.
  • No AI in class. Use of AI during in-class sessions is prohibited unless explicitly authorized by the instructor. At times, we may incorporate AI examples or exercises in class as part of building AI literacy and understanding appropriate use.

Any use of AI outside these conditions is considered an academic integrity violation and will receive zero credit. Examples include: submitting code not introduced in class as your primary solution (even if it works), failing to cite sources, omitting or writing an AI disclosure narrative that does not comply with instructions.

The bottom line: You must be the author for all work submitted in your assignments. If you don’t understand what the program is doing and are not prepared to explain it in detail (we might ask), you should not submit it.

UChicago Academic Integritiy Policy

Each student in this course is expected to abide by the University of Chicago Code of Academic Integrity. Under the provisions of the Code, anyone who gives or receives unauthorized assistance in the preparation of work at home or during tests in class will be subject to disciplinary action. A student’s name on any piece of work is our assurance that they have neither given nor received any unauthorized help in its preparation.
